Parkinson's Disease: What Happens When The Brain's Neurons Die?

April 2026·7 min read

When people hear about Parkinson's disease, the first thing that usually comes to mind is shaking hands and tremors, maybe even a well known athlete like Muhammad Ali who was diagnosed in 1984. While shaking and tremors is one of the most recognizable symptoms, Parkinson's is actually much more complex and affects millions of people worldwide in ways that often go unnoticed.

What is Parkinson's Disease?

It is a progressive neurological disorder that affect's one's movement. It develops when nerve cells in a part of the brain called the substantia nigra begin to break down or die. These cells are responsibile for producing dopamine, a chemical that helps with controlling movement and coordination. As these levels decrease, it becomes harder for the brain to send smooth signals for movement to the muscles.

The substantia nigra (SN) is a key part of motor control, dopamine production and cognition. It's involved in voluntary movement and is the primary site of neurodegeneration in Parkinson's disease.

What Causes It?

While the exact cause of Parkinson's is not fully understood. Many researchers believe it's a mix of genetics and evironmental triggers like toxins or head injuries. One of the most prominant discoveries involves a protein called alpha-synuclein. When someone has Parkinson's, this protein misfolds and clumps together causing strucutres known as Lewy bodies. These clumps disturb normal brain cell function and eventually lead to cell death. The disease targets dopamine-producing neurons. By the time many symptoms appear, scientists estimate that between 60-80% of these neurons may already be lost. Research has been done pointing to two main gene mutations, LRRK2 and SNCA. LRRK2 is a human protein-coding gene that's involved in neuronal signaling and cytoskeletal regulation while SNCA is crucial for synaptic function and implicated in neurodegenerative disorders and known for it's role in Parkinson's disease.

The first discovery of alpha synuclein and lewy bodies was made in 1997 with lead researchers being Maria Grazia Spillantini, Michel Goedert, and Ross Jakes.

Treatments

The most effective and known drug used is Levodopa. It converts into dopamine in the brain, helps reduce the tremors and stiffness. This drug is often combined with other drugs of similar nature to improve effectiveness. Long-term use of these kinds of drugs can lead to side effects including dyskinesia (involuntary movements). Lately, treatments have branched out to more advanced and technological ones. Deep brain stimulation is a procedure where electrodes are implanted into specific parts of the brain. They send electrical signals to regulate abnormal brain activity but is still oftenly used with medications. Along with medications and technological stimulations, rehabilitation therapies help patients maintain their independance longer. With technology advancing daily, scientists are actively working on new treatments like gene therapy, stem cell therapy, and neuroprotective drugs.

Living with Parkinson's

Living with Parkinson's is challenging for the patient and the people supporting them, yet many people continue to lead meaningful lives. For example, Micheal J. Foc, whose raised awareness and funded millions into research through his foundation. Stories like his help change the stigma around the disease, it isn't the end of someone's life but a condition that can be managed with support. Awareness for Parkinsons isn't just supporting patients but the thousands of researchers behind treatments. The more we talk about it, the closer we are to better treatments, the true cause and one day, the cure.

Syanptic Plasticity: How Your Brain Rewires Itself Every Day

April 2026·8 min read

Have you ever wondered how you're able to learn a new skill? memorize song lyrics? or just getting better at something? The simple answer is a practice or repitition. The long answer is a conept called synaptic plasticity, which is the brain's ability to constantly adapt and rewire itself.

What Is Synaptic Plasticity?

Synaptic plasticity refers to the ability of connections between neurons (specifically ones called synapses) to strengthen or weaken over time. These changes happen based on experiences, thoughts and actions. Everytime you learn something new, your brain is physically reshaping itself by adjusting how neurons communicate.

In simple terms, your brain isn't fixed.. it's constantly changing based on experiences, actions and thoughts.

How Does it Work?

So your brain contains billions of neurons that communication with eachother through electrical and chemical signals. When you repeat an action (like listening to the same song a couple times) or thought (like studying material for a test), certain neural pathways are used more often leading to two major processes: strengthening connections and weakening connections. When neurons consistantly activate together, their connection become stronger; this process is known as long-term potentiation (LTP). A landmark study by Tim Bliss and Terje Lømo in 1973 first demonstrated LTP in the hippocampus, a brain region crucial for memory. Think of it like creating a path in a forest, the more people use that path the clearer and easier it is to follow. On the opposite side, connections that aren't being used as often can weaken or disappear; this is called synaptic pruning. Research published in "Neuron" highlights how pruning is essential for efficient brain function and development. According to studies like those by Luo and O'Leary (2005), this pruning process is highly active during childhood and adolescence, shaping how efficiently the brain functions. Your brain is basically optimizing itself, keeping what's useful and removing what's not.

The Chemistry Behind It

Synaptic plasticity depends heavily on neurotransmitters like Glutamate and Dopamine. Gluatamate activates receptors (like NMDA) that trigger LTP. Dopamine reinforces learning by signaling reward and motivation in your brain. A major review in "Nature Reviews Neuroscience" explains how these chemical systems drive learning and memory in much more detail. This is why learning is often stronger when it's tied to emotion or reward. For example, successfully solving a problem or achieving a goal triggers dopamine release, which strengthens the neural pathways involved.

Synaptic Plasticity and Mental Health

Research by Ronald S. Duman shows that conditions like depression are associated with reduced synaptic connectivity, particularly in brain regions involved in mood and decision-making. For example, ketamine, an antidepressant, has been shown to rapidly increase synapse formation. Instead of slowly altering neurotransmitters like traditional medications, it appears to restore connectivity between neurons much more quickly. This suggests that mental health disorders may not just be "chemical imbalances" but also involve structural changes in the brain connectivity and that recovery involves rewiring those connections.

Can You Improve Your Brain's Plasticity?

Yes! You can! Studies by Carl W. Cotman show that physical activity increases levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), a protein that supports neuron growth and synaptic plasticity. This means exercise doesn't just improve your body, it literally helps your brain form stronger connections. Sleep is also critical. Research by Jan Born demonstrates that sleep helps consolidate synaptic changes made during the day, turning short-term learning into long-term memory. Without sleep, those newly formed connections may weaken or fail to stabilize.

Every thought, habit, and experience leaves a physical path in your brain. Over time, these small changes shape how you think, learn, and behave. So the next time learning feels slow or frustrating, remember that your brain isn't stuck, it's actively rewiring itself, one connection at a time.

Heart-Brain Axis: The Hidden Conversation In Your Body

April 2026·7 min read

When we think about emotions, decisions, or stress, we normally focus on the brain. But what if your heart is just as involved? The connection between your heart and brain, AKA the Heart-brain axis, is a two-way communication system that influences everything from your emotions to your physical health.

What Is The Heart-Brain Axis?

The Heart-Brain Axis is the signals consistantly being sent between the heart and brain. They communicate with eachother through a network of nerves, hormones, and electrical impluses. This communication typically happens through the autonomic nervous system, which controls automatic body functions like heart rate, breathing, and digestion. There are two main branches of this system; sympathetic nervous system which is your "flight or fright" and parasympathetic nervous system which is your "rest and digest". Your heart responds instantly to signals from the brain as does every other part of your body the heart also sends signals back which is unique to a few parts of your body.

The Main Connection

The most important connection between the brain and the heart is the Vagus nerve. Research shows that about 80% of the signals in the vagus nerve travel from the body to the brain, not the other way around. That means your heart is constantly informing your brain about what’s happening in your body.
